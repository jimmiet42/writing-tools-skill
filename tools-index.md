# Clark's 51 writing tools: condensed index

Each entry has a short principle (what the tool says) and a when-to-reach-for-it line (what trouble in the draft calls for it). For deeper context, consult the book itself: Roy Peter Clark, *Writing Tools: 50 Essential Strategies for Every Writer*.

## Table of contents

Tools 1 to 10: sentence and word craft.
Tools 11 to 20: pacing, tone, detail.
Tools 21 to 30: story and structure.
Tools 31 to 40: revision and process basics.
Tools 41 to 51: habits and long-term craft.

---

### 1. Branch to the Right

Principle. Begin sentences with subjects and verbs, letting subordinate elements branch to the right.
When to reach for it. The sentence feels muddled because the subject and verb are buried; readers wait too long to find out who did what.

### 2. Use Strong Verbs

Principle. Use verbs in their strongest form: simple present or past.
When to reach for it. Prose feels sluggish. Too many "is" and "was" constructions. The action needs punch.

### 3. Beware of Adverbs

Principle. Adverbs can dilute or repeat the verb.
When to reach for it. You catch yourself writing "ran quickly" (the verb should do the work) or leaning on "really," "very," "literally."

### 4. Period As a Stop Sign

Principle. Place strong words at the beginning and end of sentences and paragraphs.
When to reach for it. The most important word in the sentence is stranded in the middle where it loses force.

### 5. Observe Word Territory

Principle. Give key words their space. Do not repeat a distinctive word unless you mean to.
When to reach for it. A striking word has been used twice in close quarters and both uses now feel weaker.

### 6. Play with Words

Principle. Choose words the average writer avoids but the average reader understands.
When to reach for it. Vocabulary feels bland or corporate. A livelier word is available without sacrificing clarity.

### 7. Dig for the Concrete and Specific

Principle. Always get the name of the dog.
When to reach for it. The writing is abstract and the reader cannot picture it. Swap in names, numbers, places, and details.

### 8. Seek Original Images

Principle. Reject clichés and first-level creativity. Make word lists, free-associate, be surprised.
When to reach for it. You reach for a familiar metaphor. Pause and find a fresher one.

### 9. Prefer Simple to Technical

Principle. Shorter words and paragraphs at the points of greatest complexity.
When to reach for it. A dense technical passage would confuse a non-expert. Simplify most where complexity peaks.

### 10. Recognize the Roots of Stories

Principle. Notice the mythic, symbolic, and poetic in everyday news and content.
When to reach for it. You want the reader to feel an archetype (journey, return, rescue) without you naming it.

### 11. Back Off or Show Off

Principle. When the topic is most serious, understate. When it is least serious, exaggerate.
When to reach for it. Tone is mismatched to weight. A grave topic is overdramatized, or a light topic is underdone.

### 12. Control the Pace

Principle. Vary sentence length.
When to reach for it. Every sentence is about the same length. The rhythm has gone flat.

### 13. Show and Tell

Principle. Move up and down the ladder of abstraction, from concrete objects to higher meanings.
When to reach for it. The writing is all concept and no grounding, or all detail and no point.

### 14. Interesting Names

Principle. Writers are drawn to people and places with interesting names.
When to reach for it. A name, brand, or title in the draft could be pulled forward for texture.

### 15. Reveal Character Traits

Principle. Reveal character through scenes, details, and dialogue, not adjectives.
When to reach for it. You have told the reader someone is brave or kind instead of showing them being so.

### 16. Odd and Interesting Things

Principle. Put odd and interesting things next to each other.
When to reach for it. Juxtaposition would create surprise, contrast, or tension.

### 17. The Number of Elements

Principle. The count of examples in a sentence carries meaning. One focuses, two contrasts, three completes, four or more suggests a catalog.
When to reach for it. You are listing items and the count shapes the feel.

### 18. Internal Cliffhangers

Principle. Use small hooks to move readers to the next page, section, or paragraph.
When to reach for it. A long piece needs reasons to keep going; place a tease between sections.

### 19. Tune Your Voice

Principle. Voice is the most elusive effect. Read aloud to find yours.
When to reach for it. The draft doesn't sound like anyone, or it sounds like the wrong person for this piece.

### 20. Narrative Opportunities

Principle. Take advantage of chances to tell a small story inside a larger piece.
When to reach for it. You have been telling when you could be showing a short scene.

### 21. Quotes and Dialogue

Principle. Quotes state a point; dialogue advances a scene. Different tools.
When to reach for it. You are using quotes as filler, or dialogue feels lifeless.

### 22. Get Ready

Principle. Expect the unexpected. Be prepared to tell the big story.
When to reach for it. You are drafting reactively. Build stock phrases, research folders, and frames before you need them.

### 23. Place Gold Coins Along the Path

Principle. Keep readers interested by placing small rewards throughout: a surprising detail, a quote, an image.
When to reach for it. A long piece sags in the middle.

### 24. Name the Big Parts

Principle. Seeing structure is easier if you label the main sections.
When to reach for it. You are outlining a longer piece. Give each section a working name so the shape becomes visible.

### 25. Repeat

Principle. Purposeful repetition is not redundancy.
When to reach for it. A repeated word, phrase, or structure would create rhythm or emphasis.

### 26. Fear Not the Long Sentence

Principle. Long sentences have their uses. Do not flinch from them.
When to reach for it. The piece reads choppy. A long, flowing sentence would build momentum or carry a sweeping idea.

### 27. Riffing for Originality

Principle. Riff on the creative language of others.
When to reach for it. You are stuck. Borrow a structure or phrase and push it in a new direction.

### 28. Writing Cinematically

Principle. Shift focus between landscape and character, wide and close.
When to reach for it. A scene feels either claustrophobic or abstract. Mix the camera angles.

### 29. Report for Scenes

Principle. The scene is the basic unit of narrative. Scenes make the reader care.
When to reach for it. You are summarizing when a scene would carry more weight.

### 30. Write Endings to Lock the Box

Principle. All writers have a license to end. Land on a word or image that stays.
When to reach for it. The last sentence fizzles or trails off.

### 31. Parallel Lines

Principle. Pay attention to parallel structure in words, phrases, and sentences.
When to reach for it. A list or comparison reads as awkward. Parallelism often fixes it.

### 32. Let It Flow

Principle. Let the first draft come out before you judge it.
When to reach for it. You are hunting for the perfect opening and not producing anything. Lower the bar and write ugly.

### 33. Rehearsal

Principle. Procrastination can be productive. Think and talk the piece before typing.
When to reach for it. You sat down to write without having thought through the argument or scene.

### 34. Cut Big, Then Small

Principle. Precise, concise writing comes from disciplined cutting.
When to reach for it. Any draft is too long. First cut whole sections. Then cut sentences. Then cut words.

### 35. Use Punctuation

Principle. Punctuation controls pace.
When to reach for it. Pace feels wrong. Swap a period for a semicolon, or a comma for a period, and listen.

### 36. Write a Mission Statement for Your Story

Principle. Know the one-sentence answer to "what is this piece about and what should the reader do or feel?"
When to reach for it. Starting any longer piece. Use it to judge what stays and what goes.

### 37. Long Projects

Principle. Break big projects into small parts.
When to reach for it. You are overwhelmed by scale. Divide and schedule.

### 38. Polish Your Jewels

Principle. In shorter works, don't waste a syllable.
When to reach for it. Short-form copy is limp because the writer was less careful than the form demanded.

### 39. The Voice of Verbs

Principle. Choose active or passive voice deliberately, not out of habit.
When to reach for it. Passive voice is hiding the actor, or active is being forced where passive would soften appropriately.

### 40. The Broken Line

Principle. Combine storytelling with reporting by weaving scenes into explanation.
When to reach for it. A reported piece needs narrative anchors, or a narrative needs hard facts.

### 41. X-Ray Reading

Principle. Read others' work as a writer. Notice the craft beneath the surface.
When to reach for it. You are stuck. Pick a writer you admire and underline their sentence openings, verbs, and endings.

### 42. Paragraphs

Principle. Go short or long, depending on purpose.
When to reach for it. Paragraphing is uniform. Short paragraphs accelerate; long paragraphs build depth.

### 43. Self-criticism

Principle. Go from nice and easy to rough and tough.
When to reach for it. You keep letting your own draft off the hook. Force a harder read.

### 44. Save String

Principle. Save information. It could feed a bigger project later.
When to reach for it. You have material that does not fit today's piece. File it, don't delete it.

### 45. Foreshadow

Principle. Plant important clues early.
When to reach for it. A reveal feels arbitrary. Drop a seed earlier so the payoff lands.

### 46. Storytellers, Start Your Engines

Principle. Good questions drive good stories.
When to reach for it. A piece feels inert. List the questions it raises and let them pull the writing.

### 47. Collaboration

Principle. Help others in their craft so they can help you.
When to reach for it. You need a reader. Build the relationship before you need it.

### 48. Create an Editing Support Group

Principle. Build a network of friends, colleagues, editors, and coaches who give you feedback.
When to reach for it. You keep publishing in isolation. Line up readers.

### 49. Learn from Criticism

Principle. Even severe or cynical criticism can help.
When to reach for it. Feedback stings. Separate the signal from the sting.

### 50. The Writing Process

Principle. Use these tools to demystify writing. Move down the staircase: idea, collect, focus, select, order, draft, revise.
When to reach for it. You feel blocked. Locate which step is missing.

### 51. Too Many 'ings'

Principle. Beware of too many -ing verbs.
When to reach for it. Sentences pile up participles ("running, jumping, diving") and lose momentum. Rotate to simple past or present.
